# Answers to Part 1: AI assisted coding

1. >**Current Workflow:** How do you currently use AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Cursor, Claude, MCPs, Agents) to assist or generate code? Do you use them differently for AI-specific tasks (e.g., designing a RAG pipeline) versus general backend work?

I’m still somewhat skeptical about delegating the actual implementation work to AI.

I’ve used ChatGPT, Copilot, and Codex, but so far I haven’t really found situations where AI clearly speeds me up - except when I need to learn something new, like when I was getting started with React. I think that’s the key distinction: if I know nothing about a topic, AI helps me get an initial structure to work from, instead of staring at a blank page.

In general, I prefer using AI for consultation rather than for fully agentic code generation. So far, I haven’t found many cases where tools like Codex (which I use) actually made me faster, aside from those specific scenarios where I’m unfamiliar with the domain.

At the moment, I mainly rely on AI when working on frontend development.

Oh - and of course, for writing commit messages! 😄

-----

2. >**The Good & The Bad:** Where do you see the biggest value in AI-assisted development? What are the current limitations or risks, especially when building systems that will themselves be consumed by AI agents?

Honestly, I don’t feel confident yet in fully evaluating this.

I have the suspicion that the current AI-driven business model might not be sustainable in the long term, and that we could eventually see a reduction in the capabilities available to developers (and workers in general). If that happens, people who have delegated most - or all - of their work to AI agents might find themselves in a difficult position, without the underlying skills to fall back on.

I’m aware of these tools and I’ve integrated them into my workflow, but I’m not ready to abandon my own skills and consider them obsolete. That said, I’m open to changing my approach if the industry clearly and sustainably moves in that direction.

As for practical risks, I think many of the current weaknesses of AI agents will likely be addressed relatively soon. Right now, strong supervision of outputs, permissions, and behavior is necessary, but I doubt things will remain this way for long. If humans decide to make themselves replaceable by AI, they will likely find ways to overcome these current limitations.

------

3. >**The Future:** How do you envision your role as an AI Solutions Engineer evolving over the next few years? What skills do you think will matter most as LLMs get better at writing code?

I honestly have no clear idea. It feels like navigating without a map.

Sometimes I get the impression that my role might become unnecessary; other times it feels like it’s just “more of the same”, only with different labels.

I’d say I’m prepared to change direction if software engineering eventually reaches a point where human involvement is no longer needed, just like many professions have disappeared over time.

Being “prepared” doesn’t mean I already know what I’ll do next, or that it will be easy, but I’ve accepted the possibility of a radical shift.

In the short term, I don’t think that simply knowing how to write code will remain a differentiating skill - just like doing manual calculations hasn’t been for a long time. Some abilities will become baseline expectations rather than added value.

That said, this shift has arguably been happening for at least a decade already: first, the skill was knowing how to search effectively on Google and copy the right snippet; now it’s evolving into understanding what code is needed for a given specification, regardless of where it comes from, whether you wrote it yourself or it was generated by an AI system.
